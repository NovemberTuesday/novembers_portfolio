About Thesis
Viruses exhibit a rapid rate of genetic mutations, resulting in diverse variants residing in different animal species, posing potential threats to human health. Vigilance and close observation of these mutations are essential to promptly identify new virus strains or mutations, enabling a swift response to emerging diseases. Comparisons between influenza, COVID-19, and various viruses shed light on the similarities, drawing parallels with the principles of Machine Learning. ...more
This project, delved into DNA analysis, leveraging algorithms to predict outcomes by calculating similarities. The focus was on analyzing DNA patterns and features, with a particular emphasis on two RNA viruses, SARS-CoV-2 (Covid-19) and Influenza H1N1 (Swine flu). To enhance our understanding, we introduced an unknown virus for comparative analysis alongside Covid-19 and flu. The primary dataset consisted of Fasta files containing information about viral nucleotides. Utilizing these nucleotide sequences, we deciphered characteristics and patterns of the viruses, such as the number of amino acids, functional proteins, and specific nucleotides. Subsequently, decisions regarding GC%, similarity %, and differences among the viruses were derived. It's noteworthy that our approach employed unsupervised learning, which allowed us to uncover insights and patterns autonomously from the given data. ...Show less

Five potential outcomes were explored when an unknown sequence was compared with variants of influenza (H1N1) and coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2). These outcomes included:

I. Exactly the same known variant of influenza or corona.
II. Possible variant of H1N1 (a variant of influenza).
III. Possible variant of SARS-CoV-2 (a variant of Corona Virus).
IV. Less than a 50% match with both variants.
V. More than a 50% match with both variants.

After thorough research and analysis of the Fasta files from NCBI, cases corresponding to outcomes I to IV were observed. However, the 'V' case, indicating more than a 50% match with both variants, has not been identified based on our current research findings.
